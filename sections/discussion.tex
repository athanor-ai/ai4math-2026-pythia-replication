\section{Comparison and discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Comparison to existing automation}
\label{sec:discussion-comparison}

\paragraph{aesop.}
Aesop \citep{aesop2023} is the workhorse forward-search tactic in
Lean 4. \texttt{pythia} declares two named aesop rule sets
(\texttt{Pythia} and \texttt{Pythia.AnytimeValid}) and uses aesop in
stage 1 of the dispatcher chain. \texttt{pythia} is not a replacement
for aesop. It is a statistics-domain registry plus a Z3 oracle plus a
vacuous-closure audit, layered on top of aesop.

\paragraph{Sledgehammer.}
Sledgehammer \citep{paulsonblanchette2010threeyears,
blanchettesledgehammer2011} dispatches Isabelle/HOL goals to
external ATPs (Vampire, E, Z3, CVC4) and reconstructs the proof.
There is no direct Lean 4 equivalent. \texttt{pythia} addresses
the applied-mathematics case via \texttt{z3\_check} for
linear-real-arithmetic, \texttt{cvc5\_check} for the QF\_BV /
QF\_LRA fragment, and \texttt{vampire\_check} / \texttt{e\_check}
for first-order logic, with kernel-checked reconstruction inside
Lean. A general-purpose Lean Sledgehammer remains out of scope
for this paper.

\paragraph{CoqHammer.}
CoqHammer \citep{czajkakaliszyk2018coqhammer} dispatches Coq goals
to external ATPs with kernel-checked reconstruction. \texttt{z3\_check}
in \texttt{pythia} adopts the same reconstruction template
(witness from external prover, in-kernel checking via
\texttt{linarith}). CoqHammer is the design template. We do not
claim a Lean-side reimplementation of CoqHammer's full premise
selection.

\paragraph{Mathlib's existing tactic suite.}
Mathlib supplies \texttt{linarith}, \texttt{polyrith},
\texttt{nlinarith}, \texttt{positivity}, \texttt{measurability},
\texttt{bound}, \texttt{gcongr}. None of these is a statistics-domain
hammer. \texttt{stats\_ineq} is the closest \texttt{pythia} relative
of the Mathlib \texttt{bound} family. The \texttt{@[stats\_ineq]}
attribute re-tags lemmas as \texttt{@[bound]}, so the mature
\texttt{bound} dispatch picks them up automatically. \texttt{pythia} composes with all of the listed
Mathlib tactics. The fall-through chain at stage 2 of the dispatcher
combines \texttt{simp}, \texttt{omega}, \texttt{linarith}, and
\texttt{positivity}. The chain is the explicit composition.

\subsection{Drafter contribution to the corpus}
\label{sec:discussion-drafter-contribution}

The thirty-two cross-domain theorems in the library were closed
with help from a swarm of contemporary drafters: Aristotle
\citep{harmonic2025aristotle},
DeepSeek-Prover-V2 \citep{deepseekprover2024v2}, Claude Sonnet
4.6, and Claude Opus 4.6. Per-theorem drafter attribution is
recorded in the library manifest at
\texttt{tools/sim/theorem\_manifest.py}. No single drafter
closes the full corpus on its own, and the union of their
successful kernel-checked contributions is what populates the
shipped library.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion-limitations}

Three concrete gaps. (i) Domain scope. \texttt{pythia} is an
applied-mathematics library. It does not address general proof
automation across all of Lean 4. (ii) Toolchain pin. The library
ships pinned to Lean 4.28 + Mathlib v4.28.0. Mathlib evolves
quickly. Each major Mathlib release typically requires a
porting pass. (iii) Drafter dependency for restocking. A small
number of theorems in the corpus were initially closed with the
help of the proprietary Aristotle prover. \texttt{pythia} does
not depend on Aristotle at use time. The shipped library is
self-contained Lean source. End users invoke \texttt{pythia!}
without any external drafter call. New theorems may need a
drafter assist during development if no \texttt{pythia!} rung
closes them. Every shipped proof is kernel-checked and
reproducible from the library source alone.
